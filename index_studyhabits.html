Time Management

1. Plan things ahead
"By failing to prepare, you are preparing to fail"
-Benjamin Franklin

  • Invest in a planner to record deadlines, events, and appointments.
  • If visual planners are more up your alley, create a schedule on an Excel spreadsheet or draw one by hand.
  • Be sure to check with your planner and schedule every morning and right before you go to bed so that you are prepared for the next day.

2. Have study breaks and blocks

  • Pomodoro Technique: Some material may require more studying than others. In order to avoid over-exhaustion, study in blocks and follow them with short breaks.
  • The length of these blocks depend on what is most convenient and effective for you.
  • Breaks should be spent to re-energize yourself and should never be too long!
    • Go take a short walk outside. Not only will it clear your mind and get some fresh air, but also inspire you.
    • Eat a small healthy snack.
    • If you are going to check social media, make sure to keep messages off to avoid engaging in a long conversation.
  • Monitor study time with a timer.

3. Put your time to good use

  • While commuting or travelling, make sure to spend your time reviewing material or flashcards. Not only will you be surprised by the amount of knowledge you will acquire in the short amount of time; but you will also have more freetime at home to get a head start on other things in your planner or watch that show you have been meaning to catch up on.
  • Read books or articles!
    • Catch up on class readings or any other books
  • Listen to podcasts!
    • Some credible media organizations such as NPR have a number of engaging or informative podcasts.

Math

1. Get help!

  • If there is any problem that you donâ€™t understand, always go to a teacher or someone for help as soon as possible.
  • Study with a group before a test to review material and test each other.
  • Use the internet as your resource! With so many opportunities at your fingertips, try to find out more information online or ask questions on a forum.

2. PRACTICE! PRACTICE! PRACTICE!
"There is no substitute for hard work"
-Thomas Edison

  • Do as many practice problems as possible. Try to solve all the problems in the chapter review section in your textbook before every test. Start with the easy problems and slowly increase difficulty.
    • For any problems that you get wrong, note them down. Redo the problem so you know your error and the correct method.
  • Purchase or borrow extra prep books
    • Some top prep brands include Kaplan, Princeton Review, and Barrons



English

1. Use flashcards!

  • By learning more advanced vocabulary words you will enhance your writing and be better prepared for both in-class tests and admissions tests such as the SAT.
  • Flashcards can also be used to write important information about texts you have read in class. This will allow you to review a book effectively before a test so that you can retain information better or have ideas ready at hand for an essay.

2. Annotate!

  • Annotate parts of the text all the time. It will encourage you to think critically of and really connect with the text.
  • Tools:
    • Highlighters: Highlight parts of the text you think are significant or interesting. However, highlight in moderation.
    • Pencils: Use a pencil to write comments in the margins, underline key sections, or circle key words. Comments should be concise.
    • Post-its: Use post-its to write what may be too much to fit in the margins.

History

1. Connect and Develop Ideas!

  • Draw mind maps to connect different ideas and develop on them further. This will allow you to get a better and more clear understanding of historical concepts.
  • Cause and Effect charts will allow you to remember how events are linked with one another as well as their impacts on the economy, government, and society.

2. Watch videos!

  • Videos are a great supplement to studying for history. There are thousands of videos and documentaries which can help you get a deeper understanding of a particular topic, time period, or figure.
    • Crash Course
    • History Channel

Science

1. Draw!

  • Drawing diagrams is a great way to remember how things function and look.
  • In fact, some tests will ask you to label parts of a given image.
  • Use mind maps to help organize information in a very easy and visual way. This will help you understand different processes and relationships better.

2. Use flashcards!

  • Terminology is king. To make sure that you have all the terms nailed down, use flashcards to keep note of each term, its definition, and even some of its characteristics if it's more complex.
  • It is also helpful to break down terms to their roots. If you are able to recognize the root, you will be able to make connections and remember the meaning of the term.
