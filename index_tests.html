SAT What is it?

  • A 3 to 4 hour test
  • Out of 2400 points
  • 10 sections in total:
    • 3 writing (one essay + 2 multiple choice)
    • 3 critical reading
    • 3 math
    • 1 experimental section
Experimental section:
  • An extra section that is not counted to your score.
  • Either math, critical reading, or writing.
*You aren't told which section is experimental. It is up to your own judgement to determine which one it is.*

SAT Why do I need it?

It is the most common option for national college admission exams.

Not all colleges require SAT. However, it is recommended to take the SAT in order to broaden your range of available schools. You don't want to find out you're ineligible for a college because you're missing a SAT score.

SAT Subject Tests
A Step Further

  • One hour test
  • Demonstrate proficiency in a subject
  • Choose when and what specific test to take
  • May take up to 3 in one sitting


*However, it is strongly recommended that you take at most two tests in one sitting.*
*Some colleges require certain SAT Subject tests for admission or placement. Check which ones you have to take in advance!*

SAT/Subject Tests Cancellation Policy

When you feel that you didn't do so well on the SAT, you want to cancel your score. But before we can talk more about score cancellation, Paratus wants to offer those considering canceling their scores a piece of advise.

  • If it is your first time taking the SAT and you have plenty of time left to retake the test, it is recommended that you keep your first score. This will allow you to see what is your weaknesses and provide you a chance to improve.
  • Many students cancel their scores because they think they did poorly. Trust yourself and wait for the result. Worst case, you just retake it. It is good to take the SAT multiple times to guarantee that you get the best score.

For SAT:

When you are submitting your SAT score to colleges, you have several options. You can send in all the scores, send scores from a single day, or send in your superscore (higheset subscores from different test dates).

Because you have several ways to choose the score, you also have multiple options to cancel the scores.
You can't cancel each subsection, but you can cancel the whole test. SAT is administered several times throughout the year and you can just retake the SAT.

The process of cancelling score for SAT is similar to cancelling for SAT Subject tests. Please look below for the process.


For SAT Subject Tests:

If you take multiple tests in one sitting and you wish to cancel one of them, you have to cancel all. With that in mind, think carefully when you are signing up for more than one Subject test. Ask yourself if you are prepared to take more than one tests.

Sometimes College Board does allow you to cancel just one score because of calculator malfunction or other reasons. These cases are unusual, however, so read more about it in detail at CollegeBoard-Score Cancellation.


For SAT & SAT Subject tests:

If you keep your score, you can choose the score you want to send in to colleges. However, if you don't use the Score Choice, all scores will be sent to colleges automatically. Make sure you report your score in time if you don't want colleges to look at some of your scores.

You can cancel the test as you walk out of the testing room. The proctor will tell you where you need to go to cancel the score.

You can submit your request to cancel your test no later than 11:59 p.m. on the Wednesday after the test date. The request form must be sent via mail only. No phone or email.

ACT What is it?

  • Another standarized test for college admission process
  • Not as well-known on the East Coast
  • 3 1/2 hours long (with a short break)
  • Longer if you take the ACT with writing

Four Sections:

  • English- 75 Q, 45 min.
  • Mathematics- 60 Q, 60 min.
  • Reading- 40 Q, 30 min.
  • Science- 40 Q, 35 min.

Calculator permitted for mathematics section only

ACT vs. SAT Which one is right for me?

Everyone has different preferences for standardized tests.

For instance, the ACT is recommended for students able to work well under time-constraints. On the other hand, the SAT is best for students with advanced critical thinking skills.

While both tests require time-management skills, you need to pace yourself more for ACT. Both tests have their pros and cons.

  • TOEFL = Test of English as a Foreign Language

    • For students

    • To prove proficiency in English for those interested in international businesses
    • Evaluates the everyday English skills required for workplace communication

IELTS
Academic & General Language Skills Evaluation Test

  • Academic IELTS: topics/materials covered in undergraduate, graduate, or professional programs
  • General IELTS: topics and material from everyday books, newspapers and magazines.
  • Same Listening and Speaking sections for both forms
  • Different Reading and Writing sections for each form

GED "Good Enough" Diploma

  • General Educational Development
  • Other names:
    • General Equivalency Diploma
    • General Education Diploma
  • Certificate stating the individual has high school level academic skills
  • Never taken online. Must go to a particular examination center
  • "Certificate of General Education Development" given for a passing score
  • Required for applying to colleges,universities, and better jobs

GED "Test Format/Info.

  • A set of five tests:
    • Math - Quantitative & algebraic problem solving (115 min)
    • Science - Life, physical, earth, space science (90 min)
    • Social Studies - Civics and government, economics, geography, U.S., and world history (90 min)
    • Reasoning Through Language Arts - Ability to read closely, write clearly, and edit and understand written text (150 min)
  • Type of Questions:
    • Multiple choice
    • Drag and drop
    • Hot spot (select an area)
    • Fill in the blank
    • Short answer
    • Extended response
